Satti

If you find yourself in Zamboanga City or the Sulu Archipelago at six in the morning, the ultimate local experience is waking up to a steaming, fiery bowl of Satti. Visually striking and deeply comforting, Satti consists of small, charcoal-grilled skewers of beef, chicken, or liver. But unlike standard barbecue, these skewers aren't the main event—they are simply the vessels for the sauce. Satti is served by taking the grilled meats and chunks of pusô (rice cooked in woven coconut leaves) and completely drowning them in a thick, vivid red-orange, sweet, and spicy gravy. It is a heavy, sweat-inducing breakfast designed to jolt your senses and fuel you for a long day ahead.

Satti is the Mindanaoan evolution of the classic Southeast Asian satay or sate. Centuries ago, Arab, Malay, and Indonesian spice merchants sailed through the Sulu Sea, bringing Islam and their culinary traditions to the indigenous Tausug, Sama, and Zamboangueño people. While traditional Malaysian or Indonesian satay is served on a dry platter with a modest side of peanut dipping sauce, the locals in the southern Philippines reinvented the dish to suit the Filipino love for "sauce over rice."

Over time, the recipe transformed. The peanut element was dialed back, and the sauce was thickened heavily with cassava or cornstarch, turning it from a dip into a hearty soup. It was also sweetened and spiked with a massive amount of local chilies and annatto for that signature crimson color. Today, Satti houses (sattihan) are cultural institutions in western Mindanao, serving as communal gathering spots where people from all walks of life share a uniquely uncolonized, indigenous morning feast.

How to Cook

  1. Prepare the Skewers:

    In a bowl, mix the small meat cubes with the soy sauce, minced garlic, and turmeric powder. Let it marinate for at least 30 minutes. Once marinated, thread 3 to 4 pieces of meat tightly onto each soaked bamboo skewer. Grill over hot charcoal for 3-5 minutes on each side until cooked with a slight char. Set aside.

  2. Extract the Color:

    Squeeze and rub the annatto (atsuete) seeds in the warm water until the water turns a deep, dark red. Strain the liquid and discard the seeds. Set the red annatto water aside.

  3. Sauté the Spice Base:

    In a large pot, heat a little oil over medium heat. Sauté the minced onion, garlic, ginger, and pounded lemongrass until highly aromatic and softened. Add the minced bird's eye chilies and cook for another minute.

  4. Build the Broth:

    Pour the beef or chicken broth into the pot, followed by the deep red annatto water. Bring the mixture to a rolling boil. Reduce the heat and stir in the peanut butter and brown sugar. Whisk continuously until the peanut butter dissolves completely and the broth becomes smooth. Let it simmer for 10 minutes to allow the flavors to meld. Season with a little salt if needed.

  5. Thicken and Serve:

    While the broth is gently simmering, slowly pour in the cornstarch slurry while stirring continuously. Keep stirring until the sauce becomes thick, glossy, and sticky (similar to the consistency of a thick gravy or sweet and sour sauce). To serve, place chopped pieces of pusô (or a mound of rice) and a few grilled skewers in a deep bowl. Ladle a generous, overflowing amount of the hot, spicy red sauce directly over the meat and rice. Eat immediately!
