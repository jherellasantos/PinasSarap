OVERVIEW

Filipino sweets are deeply connected to local ingredients like coconut, sticky rice, and palm sugar. This category highlights beloved desserts and kakanin that are often served during gatherings, holidays, and merienda. From soft and chewy rice cakes to caramelized treats, these dishes represent the comforting and festive side of Filipino cuisine.

FILIPINO SWEETS

SUMAN MALAGKIT

Suman Malagkit is a traditional Filipino rice cake (a type of kakanin). It is made from glutinous (sticky) rice cooked in coconut milk, sugar, and salt, then wrapped in banana leaves or buri palm leaves and steamed. It is commonly eaten as a snack, dessert, or even breakfast, and can be served plain or with toppings like sugar, coconut, or caramel sauce.

UBE HALAYA

Ube Halaya, which is associated with the Visayas, is a rich Filipino purple yam jam cooked with milk, butter, and sugar until smooth and creamy. It is commonly served as a dessert or used as a filling in other sweet treats.

BIBINGKA

Bibingka, which originated in Luzon, is a classic Filipino rice cake traditionally baked during the Christmas season. It is soft, slightly smoky, and often topped with salted egg, cheese, and grated coconut.

LECHE FLAN

Leche Flan, which is closely associated with Luzon, is a rich steamed custard made with egg yolks, milk, and sugar, finished with a golden caramel topping. It is smooth, creamy, and a staple dessert at Filipino gatherings.

HALO-HALO

Halo-Halo, which is widely associated with Luzon, is a famous Filipino shaved ice dessert with mixed sweet ingredients, milk, and ice cream or leche flan on top. It is colorful, refreshing, and perfect for hot days.
