Tiyula Itum

Tiyula Itum is a distinctive, striking black beef or goat soup native to the Tausug people of the Sulu Archipelago in Mindanao. Visually, its dark, almost ink-black broth is instantly recognizable. The color and unique roasted, earthy flavor come from pamapa itum, a burnt coconut spice paste. Unlike standard clear broths, Tiyula Itum is deeply savory, slightly spicy, and aromatic, often described as a richer, smokier, and more complex cousin to the classic Filipino nilaga. It is a comforting, warming soup that perfectly balances the smokiness of charred coconut with the richness of tender, slow-cooked meat.

Tiyula Itum literally translates to "black soup" in the Tausug language. It holds a highly prestigious place in southern Philippine cuisine, traditionally considered a dish for royalty and grand celebrations. Historically, it was served during weddings, religious festivals like Eid al-Fitr, and grand banquets to welcome high-ranking guests of the Sulu Sultanate. The ingenious use of roasting coconut meat until it is pitch black before pounding it with aromatics reflects the Tausug's mastery of their local agricultural resources. It showcases the heavy influence of the ancient Malay and Indonesian spice trade in the region, transformed into a culinary masterpiece entirely unique to the Tausug culture.

How to Cook

  1. Make the Pamapa Itum:

    Heat a dry pan or wok over medium heat. Add the grated coconut and toast it continuously until it turns pitch black (make sure it is deeply charred, but do not let it turn into white ash).

  2. Pound the paste:

    Transfer the blackened coconut into a mortar and pestle. Add the minced garlic, ginger, turmeric, lemongrass, and chilies. Pound everything together until it forms a thick, dark, and aromatic spice paste. Set aside..

  3. Sear the meat:

    In a large, heavy-bottomed pot, heat the cooking oil over medium-high heat. Sauté the crushed garlic, sliced onion, and sliced ginger until aromatic and softened.

  4. Coat with spices:

    Add the beef cubes to the pot and sear until lightly browned. Spoon in about 3 to 4 tablespoons of your prepared pamapa itum paste. Stir well so the meat is completely coated in the black paste. Cook for 3 to 5 minutes to release the oils and aromas of the spices.

  5. Simmer:

    Pour the water or beef broth into the pot. Bring the liquid to a rolling boil, then reduce the heat to low. Cover the pot and let it simmer for 1.5 to 2 hours, or until the beef is incredibly tender. (Add a little more water during the process if the broth reduces too much).

  6. Season and serve:

    Once the meat is fork-tender, season the black broth with salt and pepper to taste. Serve piping hot in a bowl alongside steamed white rice.
